# Command: /pr:assess-news-value

## Description
Evaluate an announcement against 14 professional newsworthiness criteria and produce a clear assessment with recommendations.

## When to Use
Run this as the first step on any new announcement. If news value is weak, address it before investing in press release writing or pitching.

## Instructions

1. Ask the user to provide: a brief description of the announcement, or a draft press release if one exists.

2. Score each of the 14 newsworthiness criteria below as **High / Medium / Low** with one sentence of rationale.

---

## The 14 Newsworthiness Criteria

**1. Timeliness**
Is this happening now, or tied to a current event, season, or trend? News that is immediate or timely outperforms evergreen announcements. A story pegged to something already in the news cycle gets a lift.
- High: Happening now or directly tied to a breaking trend
- Medium: Recent but not urgent; no clear news peg
- Low: Could have been announced any time; no timeliness signal

**2. Magnitude / Impact**
How many people are affected, and how significantly? Scale matters. An announcement affecting 10,000 people is more newsworthy than one affecting 100.
- High: Large number of people significantly affected
- Medium: Moderate scale or modest impact on a large audience
- Low: Small audience, limited impact

**3. Novelty**
Is this genuinely new — first of its kind, a record, a breakthrough? Journalists cover what's new. "First," "largest," "only," and "new" are powerful if defensible.
- High: Demonstrably first, only, or record-setting
- Medium: New for this organization but not the industry
- Low: Incremental; similar announcements have been made before

**4. Proximity**
Is this relevant to the journalist's local audience or geographic beat? Local angles unlock local coverage that national pitches miss.
- High: Strong local/regional relevance to target outlet's audience
- Medium: Some geographic connection but not the primary angle
- Low: No meaningful geographic hook

**5. Human Interest**
Is there a relatable human story here — a real person affected, a compelling struggle, an emotional dimension? Data alone rarely drives feature coverage; human stories do.
- High: Clear human protagonist, emotional resonance, relatable stakes
- Medium: Human element present but not central
- Low: Primarily institutional or technical; no human story

**6. Conflict / Tension**
Is there a problem being solved, an adversary being overcome, a debate being resolved? Conflict is narrative fuel. Journalists are drawn to tension.
- High: Clear antagonist, obstacle, or unresolved tension that this announcement addresses
- Medium: Mild problem-solution framing
- Low: No conflict; purely positive institutional announcement

**7. Prominence**
Does this involve well-known people, brands, or institutions? Name recognition attracts coverage. A partnership with a recognized brand amplifies newsworthiness.
- High: Involves nationally or globally recognized name, brand, or institution
- Medium: Known within the industry but not broadly
- Low: No prominent names or brands involved

**8. Currency**
Is this topic already generating media attention? Announcements that ride existing media momentum get easier coverage than those introducing entirely new topics.
- High: Topic is actively in the news cycle; journalists are already writing about this space
- Medium: Topic has been covered recently but is not actively trending
- Low: Topic is not in current media conversation; requires education before pitching

**9. Consequence**
What happens if people ignore this? Stakes amplify newsworthiness. If the audience has something to lose by not knowing this, journalists have reason to cover it.
- High: Meaningful consequences for inaction or ignorance — financial, safety, behavioral
- Medium: Useful to know but low personal stakes
- Low: No material consequence to the audience for not knowing

**10. Actionability**
Can the audience do something with this information? Actionable news — where the reader can take a step, make a decision, or change behavior — performs better than purely informational announcements.
- High: Clear, immediate action the audience can take based on this news
- Medium: Mildly actionable; awareness is the primary goal
- Low: Purely informational; no action implied

**11. Visual / Multimedia Potential**
Does this story have strong visual elements — compelling images, video opportunities, data visualizations, demonstrations? Broadcast and digital outlets favor stories that show well.
- High: Strong visual elements available (event footage, product demo, striking data viz, compelling location)
- Medium: Some visual assets available but not central to the story
- Low: Text-only story; limited visual potential

**12. Exclusivity**
Is this information only available here and now? Exclusives and embargoes create urgency. Being first matters to journalists.
- High: Exclusive data, first-look access, or information not available elsewhere
- Medium: Not exclusive but we are the authoritative source
- Low: Information is or will soon be publicly available from multiple sources

**13. Audience Relevance**
How directly does this news affect the specific readership of target outlets? Relevance to the journalist's audience is the single biggest predictor of pickup. This is distinct from topic relevance — it's about who reads and why they care.
- High: Announcement directly affects the daily life, work, or decisions of the target outlet's audience
- Medium: Interesting to the audience but not directly affecting them
- Low: Tangential to the audience's interests or concerns

**14. Source Credibility**
Does the organization's reputation and track record lend authority to the announcement? Credibility reduces journalist skepticism and increases pickup probability.
- High: Organization is a recognized authority in this space with track record of reliable information
- Medium: Organization is known but not the definitive authority on this topic
- Low: Organization is unknown to journalists or has credibility challenges to overcome

---

## Overall Assessment

After scoring all 14 criteria, produce an overall rating:

- **Strong (10+ criteria at Medium or High, with 4+ at High)**: Pitch broadly, pursue top-tier targets, consider exclusives
- **Moderate (6-9 criteria at Medium or High)**: Pitch selectively, focus on trade and niche outlets most likely to care, sharpen the angle
- **Weak (fewer than 6 criteria at Medium or High)**: Identify what would strengthen the story before pitching; recommend holding or reworking

## Output
Deliver a concise news value report with:
1. Scores and one-line rationale for all 14 criteria
2. Overall rating with recommendation
3. The single strongest angle — the most compelling reason a journalist would cover this
4. The 2-3 criteria with the most room for improvement, and specific suggestions for each
5. Any red flags that could undercut coverage even if the story is pitched
6. Recommended next step (competitive scan, release review, or hold/rework)
