# Pitch Drafting Skill

## Purpose
Write personalized, high-converting pitch emails for each journalist tier. Prioritize specificity and brevity over comprehensiveness. The goal is to earn a reply, not deliver a press kit.

---

## The Anatomy of an Effective Pitch

### Subject Line
This is the most important sentence in the pitch. If it doesn't earn an open, nothing else matters.

Rules:
- Under 60 characters where possible (mobile-friendly)
- Specific, not clever. "Chicago Transit Ridership Hits 10-Year High" beats "Big News from Metra"
- Contains the actual news signal — number, name, or outcome
- Does not start with "PRESS RELEASE:" or "FOR IMMEDIATE RELEASE"
- Avoid: "Following up," "Checking in," "Exclusive opportunity," "Important announcement"
- Test against this question: Would a journalist reading 200 emails open this one?

Generate 3-5 subject line options for every pitch. Let the user choose.

### Opening Line — The Hook
First sentence must answer: "Why should this journalist care about this, right now?"

Do NOT open with:
- "My name is [X] and I represent [Organization]..."
- "I hope this email finds you well..."
- "I wanted to reach out about..."
- A summary of the press release

DO open with:
- The most newsworthy fact, stated plainly
- A reference to the journalist's recent work that connects to this story
- A data point or statistic that signals the scope of the story
- A human consequence that makes the abstract concrete

### Personalization Block (2-3 sentences)
For Tier A journalists, include a genuine personalization signal:
- Reference a specific article they wrote (by topic, not just title)
- Connect their established beat to this story specifically
- Note their audience and why this story serves that audience

This section should feel like it was written for this journalist specifically. If it could be copy-pasted to any journalist, it's not personalized enough.

### The News (3-5 sentences)
Communicate the essential facts:
- What is happening
- Who is involved
- Why it matters (impact, scale, consequence)
- What's unique about it (USP, briefly)

This is not a press release summary. It's the journalist's version of the story, compressed into a paragraph.

### The Ask (1-2 sentences)
Be specific about what you want:
- "I'd love to offer you an exclusive interview with [Spokesperson] before the release goes out on [Date]."
- "Happy to provide the full data set, including [specific interesting subset], if you'd like to dig deeper."
- "Could we set up a 15-minute call this week?"

Never end a pitch without a clear, low-friction ask.

### Close
One sentence. Professional, not sycophantic.

Avoid: "I look forward to hearing from you and hope we can work together on this exciting story!"
Better: "Happy to send the full release or set up a call at your convenience."

---

## Pitch Length Guidelines

| Tier | Recommended Length |
|------|-------------------|
| Tier A exclusive | 200-300 words (more personalization, more depth) |
| Tier A standard | 150-250 words |
| Tier B | 100-175 words |
| Tier C template | 75-125 words |

If a pitch exceeds these ranges, cut — don't add. Journalists respond better to brevity.

---

## Segmented Pitch Strategy

Do not write one pitch for all journalists. Segment by:

**By beat/angle:**
- Business/financial press: lead with economic impact, data, market implications
- Consumer/general press: lead with human interest, community impact, accessibility
- Trade/industry press: lead with technical detail, operational significance, industry benchmarks
- Local/regional press: lead with geographic specificity, local employment, community benefit
- Broadcast: lead with visual opportunity, spokesperson availability, human story

**By tier:**
- Tier A: fully personalized, specific reference to journalist's work, exclusive or advance access offered
- Tier B: angle-segmented but less individualized
- Tier C: template with merge fields for minimal personalization

---

## Quality Checklist Before Finalizing

- [ ] Subject line is specific and under 60 characters
- [ ] Opening line does not start with "I" or the organization name
- [ ] Personalization is genuine, not generic
- [ ] The most newsworthy fact appears in the first two sentences
- [ ] The USP is clearly communicated
- [ ] There is a specific, actionable ask
- [ ] Length is appropriate for the tier
- [ ] Tone matches the outlet (tabloid vs. trade vs. national broadsheet require different registers)
- [ ] No spelling errors or wrong journalist names (especially if using templates)

---

## Output Format
For each journalist segment, produce:
1. 3-5 subject line options with brief rationale
2. Full pitch draft
3. One-sentence note on the strategic intent of this pitch (why this angle for this journalist)
