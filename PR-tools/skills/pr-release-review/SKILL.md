# Press Release Review Skill

## Purpose
Audit or draft a press release to ensure it contains the right key messages, supporting points, structural elements, and newsworthiness signals that will hold up under journalist scrutiny and support an effective pitch.

---

## Standard Press Release Structure

Every press release should follow this structure. Flag any missing element.

1. **Headline** — Specific, active voice, news-forward. Should work as a standalone tweet.
2. **Dateline** — City, State, Date
3. **Lead paragraph** — Who, what, when, where, why. Must answer the journalist's core question in one paragraph. No throat-clearing.
4. **Second paragraph** — The "so what." Why does this matter? What's the impact or implication?
5. **Supporting body paragraphs** — Proof points, data, context, supporting facts. Should flow from most to least important.
6. **Quote block 1** — Primary spokesperson. Should add perspective or emotion, not restate facts. Avoid generic enthusiasm.
7. **Quote block 2 (if applicable)** — Partner, customer, or third-party validator. Most credible when it comes from outside the organization.
8. **Boilerplate** — Standard "About [Organization]" paragraph. Should be current and accurate.
9. **Media contact** — Name, phone, email. Must be a reachable human, not a generic inbox.

---

## Review Criteria

### Headline
- [ ] Contains the actual news (not a teaser or marketing phrase)
- [ ] Under 100 characters preferred
- [ ] Active voice
- [ ] Avoids superlatives ("first ever," "revolutionary," "game-changing") unless verifiable
- [ ] Would make sense to a reader with no prior context

### Lead Paragraph
- [ ] Answers who, what, when, where in the first two sentences
- [ ] Does not start with "Today, [Organization] is pleased to announce..."
- [ ] News hook is in the first sentence, not buried
- [ ] Under 75 words

### Body Content
- [ ] Key messages are present and in priority order (most important first)
- [ ] All factual claims are sourced or verifiable
- [ ] Data is specific, not vague ("reduced costs by 23%" not "significantly reduced costs")
- [ ] No jargon that would require the journalist to translate for their audience
- [ ] Human interest or impact angle is present (who benefits and how)

### Quotes
- [ ] Quotes sound human, not like they were written by a committee
- [ ] Quote adds perspective or emotion beyond what the text already says
- [ ] Spokesperson title is correct and current
- [ ] Second quote (if present) comes from a credible third party

### Key Messages Audit
List the intended key messages and check each one:
- Is it present in the release?
- Is it supported by at least one proof point?
- Is it competitive — i.e., not something any competitor could say?
- Is it in language a journalist would use with their audience?

### Boilerplate
- [ ] Accurate and current
- [ ] Concise (3-5 sentences maximum)
- [ ] Includes website URL

---

## Red Flags — Flag These Immediately
- Unverified superlatives ("first," "only," "largest") without sourcing
- Forward-looking statements without appropriate hedging
- Missing or incorrect attribution for statistics
- Quotes that sound like marketing copy
- Buried lead (the most newsworthy fact is not in the first paragraph)
- Missing call to action or next step for the reader
- Embargo information missing when applicable

---

## Output Format
When reviewing a press release, produce:
1. **Overall assessment** (2-3 sentences): Is this ready to pitch, needs revision, or needs significant rework?
2. **Structural checklist results** — flag any missing or weak elements
3. **Key messages audit table** — for each intended message: present/missing, supported/unsupported, competitive/generic
4. **Specific revision recommendations** — numbered list, prioritized by importance
5. **Revised headline options** (3 alternatives if current headline is weak)
6. **Revised lead paragraph** (if current lead is weak)

When drafting a new press release, apply all criteria above from the start and produce a complete draft ready for stakeholder review.
